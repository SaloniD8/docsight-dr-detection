Disease Overview
Diabetic Retinopathy
The leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults worldwide. Early detection saves sight.
What is Diabetic Retinopathy?

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a diabetes complication that affects the eyes. It is caused by damage to the blood vessels of the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye (retina). At first, diabetic retinopathy may cause no symptoms or only mild vision problems. Eventually it can cause blindness. The condition can develop in anyone who has type 1 or type 2 diabetes.

5 Stages of Diabetic Retinopathy
🟢
No DR
No signs of retinopathy. Annual screening recommended.
🔵
Mild NPDR
Small microaneurysms in the retinal blood vessels.
🟡
Moderate NPDR
Blood vessels that nourish the retina are blocked.
🟠
Severe NPDR
Many blocked blood vessels. Body signals to grow new ones.
🔴
Proliferative DR
New fragile vessels grow and can leak blood. Severe vision loss risk.

NPDR = Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy. This tool screens for presence or absence of DR.

Symptoms to Watch For
  • Spots or dark strings floating in your vision (floaters)
  • Blurred or fluctuating vision throughout the day
  • Dark or empty areas in your vision
  • Difficulty with colour perception
  • Vision loss — often no early warning signs
  • Sudden vision changes after blood sugar fluctuation
⚠️ Important: Early DR often has NO symptoms. Regular screening is essential even if your vision seems fine.

Treatment Options
💊
Early Stage

Blood sugar, blood pressure and cholesterol management. Regular monitoring every 6–12 months.

💉
Moderate Stage

Anti-VEGF injections (Ranibizumab, Bevacizumab) to prevent abnormal vessel growth. Laser photocoagulation.

🔬
Advanced Stage

Vitrectomy surgery to remove blood from eye. Scatter laser treatment for proliferative DR.

Preventive Care
How to Take Care of Your Eyes
Evidence-based habits that protect your vision and reduce risk of eye disease throughout your life.
🩺

Get Regular Eye Exams

Adults should have a comprehensive eye exam every 1–2 years even without symptoms. People with diabetes need annual dilated eye exams. Many serious eye diseases have no early symptoms — only an exam can detect them.

🩸

Control Blood Sugar (for Diabetics)

Keeping HbA1c below 7% reduces the risk of developing diabetic retinopathy by up to 76%. Monitor your blood glucose daily and follow your doctor's medication plan. This is the single most important thing you can do if you have diabetes.

💻

Follow the 20-20-20 Rule for Screen Time

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This reduces digital eye strain. Keep screens at arm's length, reduce glare, and increase text size to avoid straining.

☀️

Wear UV-Protective Sunglasses

UV radiation from the sun increases risk of cataracts and macular degeneration. Wear sunglasses that block 99–100% of UVA and UVB rays whenever outdoors, even on cloudy days. A wide-brimmed hat provides additional protection.

🚬

Quit Smoking

Smoking doubles the risk of macular degeneration and significantly increases cataract risk. It also worsens diabetic retinopathy by narrowing blood vessels. Quitting at any age reduces eye disease risk substantially.

😴

Get Adequate Sleep

Eyes repair themselves during sleep. Insufficient sleep causes dry eyes, eye spasms, and blurred vision. Aim for 7–9 hours. If you have persistent dry eyes or tearing, see an ophthalmologist — it may indicate a tear duct issue.

🥽

Use Protective Eyewear

Wear safety glasses or goggles when working with tools, chemicals, or playing contact sports. Eye injuries are a leading cause of preventable vision loss. Sports goggles are recommended for racquet sports, basketball, and swimming.

Disease Library
Common Eye Diseases
Understanding the most prevalent eye conditions — their causes, symptoms, and treatment options.
👁
Glaucoma
Silent Blinding Disease Affects 80 million people worldwide
What it is

A group of eye diseases that damage the optic nerve, usually caused by abnormally high pressure in the eye (intraocular pressure). Often called the "silent thief of sight" because it has no symptoms until significant vision is lost. Once lost, vision cannot be restored.

Symptoms
  • Gradual loss of peripheral (side) vision
  • Tunnel vision in advanced stages
  • Severe eye pain (acute angle-closure)
  • Headache, nausea with eye pain
  • Halos around lights
  • Often NO symptoms until late
Treatment
  • Eye drops to lower eye pressure
  • Oral medications (Acetazolamide)
  • Laser therapy (trabeculoplasty)
  • Surgical drainage procedures
  • Regular IOP monitoring
  • Lifelong management required
🌫️
Cataracts
Most Common Cause of Blindness Affects 95 million people globally
What it is

A clouding of the normally clear lens of the eye. The lens sits behind the iris and pupil and works much like a camera lens. As cataract progresses, the clouding becomes denser and interferes more with vision. Most are age-related but can occur at any age.

Symptoms
  • Clouded, blurred or dim vision
  • Increased difficulty seeing at night
  • Sensitivity to light and glare
  • Seeing halos around lights
  • Frequent changes in glass prescription
  • Fading or yellowing of colours
Treatment
  • Early: stronger glasses, magnification
  • Phacoemulsification surgery (gold standard)
  • Artificial intraocular lens implant
  • Usually outpatient — 15 min procedure
  • Success rate over 95%
  • Fully reversible with surgery
🔍
Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)
Leading Cause in 50+ 196 million affected worldwide by 2020
What it is

A condition that affects the macula — the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, straight-ahead vision. AMD doesn't cause total blindness but can make it very hard to read, drive, or recognise faces. There are two forms: dry (more common) and wet (more severe).

Symptoms
  • Blurred or reduced central vision
  • Need for brighter light when reading
  • Difficulty adapting to low light
  • Straight lines appear wavy (wet AMD)
  • Blank spot in central vision
  • Colours appear less vivid
Treatment
  • Dry AMD: AREDS2 vitamin supplements
  • Wet AMD: Anti-VEGF injections
  • Photodynamic therapy
  • Low vision aids and rehabilitation
  • Lifestyle: quit smoking, eat greens
  • No cure — management focused
💧
Dry Eye Syndrome
Very Common Affects 1 in 5 adults; rising with screen use
What it is

Dry eye occurs when the tear glands don't make enough tears or the tears evaporate too quickly. This leads to inflammation and damage of the eye surface. It is increasingly common due to prolonged screen use, air conditioning, and contact lens wear.

Symptoms
  • Stinging, burning or scratchy sensation
  • Stringy mucus in or around eyes
  • Sensitivity to light
  • Watery eyes (reflex tearing)
  • Blurred vision, eye fatigue
  • Difficulty wearing contact lenses
Treatment
  • Artificial tear eye drops
  • Prescription drops (Cyclosporine)
  • Warm compresses on eyelids
  • Omega-3 fatty acid supplements
  • Punctal plugs for severe cases
  • Reduce screen time, use humidifier
